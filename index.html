About Me

Julio M. Morales

My name is Julio (Manuel) Morales, welcome to my website! I'm a first-generation Puerto Rican college graduate, a proud member of the LGBTQIA+ community, and an aspiring Astrophysicist. This page is for you to get to know a bit about me and my academic career.

My Academic Journey

Like many in the field, I was inspired to pursue Astronomy at an early age. I can remember being 6 years old—looking for something interesting to watch on TV. I eventually stumbled across a show called "The Universe" on the National Geographic Channel. I was instantly enamoured—the shear size of celestial bodies and astronomical distances captured my imagination (see Figure 1), and I couldn't stop watching Astronomy shows and documentaries.

I spent months watching every show related to Astronomy that I could find, and it didn't take long before I realized that I wanted to be an Astronomer. I understood that I would have to go to college, so I snuck on my mothers Windows XP computer and googled "astronomy school". I read about UMass Amherst—a school for Astronomy where they made some big discoveries at the time. Naturally, I told myself that I would one day go there. Fast forward 13 years and I was accepted into the Astronomy Department at UMass Amherst!

4 years after that, and I finished my Bachelors of Science in Astonomy and Physics in the spring of 2022 (see my CV). I will continue my studies at New Mexico State University where I will be working on my Ph.D with Professor Jason Jackiewicz on the solar meridional flow using helioseismology. I hope to eventually become a Professor of Astronomy at a small liberal arts college, so that I may continue research in the field, and lead my own lab of (primarily) undergraduate students.

Research

Undergraduate Research Assistant (2021-2022)

I've had the privellege of being able to conduct formal research in Astronomy. In the summer of 2021, I joined the lab of Professor Follette at Amherst College, where I learned about the beautifully complex young stellar objects known as T-Tauri stars.

Accretion in T-Tauri Stars

These are young, low-mass stars in a phase of formation characterized by accretion on to the star from the circumstellar environment. T-Tauri stars possess strong magnetic fields (several kilogauss) owing to their convective outer envelopes. In fact, their fields are so strong that they can interupt the Keplerian orbit of the inner-disk material. The material flows along the stellar magnetic field lines and crashes on to the photosphere where it shocks and produces X-ray, UV, and optical emission (see Figure 2).

Protoplanetary Disk Chemistry

The accretion process is the primary source of ionizing photons in a planetary systems early life, and is believed to influence the chemical and physical properties of the protoplanetary disk (see Figure 3). Studying the accretion process is the ground work to a deterministic model of planet formation.

Accretion Variability

Photometric variability is a defining characteristic of T-Tauri stars (see Figure 4). There are countless sources of variability, but my research has focused on accretion variability. The accretion column on a star produces a hotspot which varies in brightness depending on the mass accretion rate. For this reason, we can study how variable the accretion process is by performing time-domain studies on the light-curves of T-Tauri stars. Such studies can shed light on the structure of the inner-disk and the accretion flows.

Transitional Disks

My project was concerned with a subset of T-Tauri stars with large cavities carved out in their centers—transitional disks (see Figure 5). These cavities are thought to be formed by embedded accreting protoplanets. Owing to their distinct cavities, transitional disks were suspected to have different accretion patterns as opposed to their full-disk counterparts, but no significant differences have been identified yet. We also have a deficit in our understanding of the timescales for accretion variability in transitional disks due to a lack of time-domain studies. Transitional disks are also very good candiates for direct imaging of accreting protoplanets since they are not enshrouded by the protostellar natal envelope. This is why I was assigned with producing photometric ratio light-curves for a survey of the 15 most well-studied transitional disks (Giant Accreting Protoplanet Survey).

Senior Thesis

For my senior thesis, I wrote an original pipeline in Python that automatically produces optimized aperture photometry. I accomplished this by modeling the point-spread-function (PSF) of the central star (see Figure 6) to set an optimal aperture size that maximizes the signal-to-noise ratio for every photometric datapoint in every dataset for both the Hα line (a popular accretion tracer) and the nearby continuum. My pipeline is also written to handle saturated images, for which the photometry is extracted using a ghost image (see Figure 7). The data I work with is ground-based, and therefore dominated by the Earth’s atmosphere. We can mitigate its affects by taking the ratio of the Hα and continuum fluxes. Since the two lines are close in wavelength space, they should be affected in the same way by the atmosphere. We expect accreting objects to be brighter in Hα, so plotting the ratio gives us a gauge for the relative brightness of the star due to accretion.

Second-to-Minute Variability

I visually sorted the light-curves by those that display significant variability across a single night of data. Since most of the data in GAPlanetS were taken with a cadence of ∼ 2.5 seconds over a few hours, this will amount to sorting the light-curves that show significant second-to-minute variability. I found that none of the datasets show signifcant variability on the second-to-minute timescale (see Figure 8). The only source of variability we should expect on the second-to-minute timescale is due to a model-dependent oscillating shock front in the accretion column on the central star. I assert that by taking the Hα/Cont ratio, we bypass continuum shock emission which is why we see no variability. Alternatively, the ampltitude of the shock oscillations could just be too small to detect, and is lost in the errorbars of the data. Changes in mass-infall rate is the only souce of variability in the Hα/Cont ratios to which we are sensitive. However, changes in mass infall are limited to the free-fall timescale (a couple of hours for most stars). The longest time-series in GAPlanetS is about 2.5 hours—too short to observe changes in mass-infall, which may also be why we see no variability.

Day-to-Day Variability

Several datasets for which data was acquired for consecutive days show signficant variaiblity in their Hα/Cont ratios (see Figure 9). Day-to-day changes such as these are consistent with changes in mass accretion rate since enough time has passed for the magnetosphere to sweep through different parts of a clumpy, inhomogenous disk.

Year-to-Year Variability

A few datasets show significant variability on the year-to-year timescale (see Figure 10). The object with the largest changes in GAPlanetS is TW Hya, which shows approximately an 21% decrease in Hα over 3 years. Such changes on the year-to-year timescale are consistent with large scale mass-density gradients in the inner-disk.

Main Results

  1. We can produce ground-based photometry that is adequately unaffected by the Earth’s atmosphere.
  2. Better characterized ghost photometry method.
  3. I found that all of the datasets in GAPlanetS lack observable second-to-minute variability.
  4. The lack of significant variability on the second-to-minute timescale validates the method of using a single median Hα/Cont ratio to directly image planets.
  5. PDS 70 system shows day-to-day variability-consistent with changes in mass accretion rate.
  6. TW Hya system shows year-to-year variability-consistent with large scale changes in inner-disk.

Outreach

Explore the Solar System: The Amazing Science & History of the Heavens

Public outreach is a fun way to get the general population interested in astronomy. There is nothing like the look of awe and curiosity on someones face when learning about the cosmos. It's the same feeling I have for it to this day, and it's also why I'm so passionate about outreach. In the summer of 2021, I organized and ran the event: "Explore the Solar System: The Amazing Science & History of the Heavens"

Electrocuting a Pickle: Emission Spectra

More than 100 participants attended my event to take part in various experiments that demonstrated astronomical phenomena. The experiment imaged above demonstrates emission lines by electrocuting a pickle (see Figure 11). The pickle glows and particpants were able to observe sodium emission lines using a cardboard spectrograph.

Fluid Differentiation: Planet Formation

Our planetary formation experiment demonstrated how actively forming, molten planets layer themselves (see Figure 12). We did this by showing the exact same process in a beaker with liquids of varying density such as oil, rubbing alcohol, and dish soap.

Scale of the Solar System: Run Faster Than the Speed of Light

Scale of the solar system had participants attempt to calculate the scaled distance between solar system bodies in the case where the Sun is scaled down to the size of a 9 inch radius ball (see Figure 13). Participants then raced across the solar system model, technically travelling faster than the speed of light!

Solar Telescope: Sunspots

Participants had the oppurtunity to observe Sunspots (see Figure 14) directly using the Celestron Solar Telescope, generously provided by the Department of Physics and Astronomy at Amherst College.

The Follette Lab

I couldn't have run this event without the help of my fellow Follete Lab members. They helped me set up and run the entire event, and I'll forever be grateful for their help and friendship!
